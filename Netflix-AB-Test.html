01 — The Business Problem

Why Netflix Obsesses Over a Single Image

When you open Netflix, you see a grid of titles. Each one is represented by a single piece of artwork — a thumbnail. Netflix's own research has found that a viewer typically decides whether to click a title in under two seconds. That thumbnail is often the only thing standing between a title being watched or ignored forever.

The question is: which version of that image makes more people click? A documentary might perform better with a bold portrait of its subject, or with a dramatic landscape scene. A thriller might do better with a close-up of a face showing fear, or with an image of the antagonist. There is no obvious right answer — and what works in one country or for one demographic may flop for another.

Netflix's solution is to never guess. Instead, they run controlled experiments — A/B tests — continuously across their platform, serving different images to different groups of real users, measuring who clicks, and letting the data decide. This project simulates exactly that process.

The Scale of the Problem

Netflix serves over 300 million subscribers across 190+ countries. A single percentage point improvement in click-through rates translates into millions of additional plays per day. Small improvements compound into enormous business value.

Why Not Just Pick the Best-Looking Image?

Human intuition about what looks compelling is notoriously unreliable. What the design team finds appealing and what a viewer in São Paulo or Seoul finds appealing can be entirely different things. Data beats opinion at scale.

The Role of Machine Learning

A/B testing is the foundation. On top of it, Netflix layers machine learning models that learn which artwork works for which viewer — personalizing the thumbnail each user sees based on their watch history and behavior. Experimentation feeds the model.

02 — The Method

What Is A/B Testing, and Why Does It Work?

An A/B test is one of the simplest and most powerful tools in data science. You take a group of people, randomly split them into two groups, show each group a different version of something, and measure which version produces better results. Because the split is random, any difference you observe in behavior between the two groups can be attributed to the thing you changed — not to any pre-existing differences between the people.

This randomness is the critical ingredient. Without it, you might show the new artwork to users who were already more likely to click — and incorrectly conclude the artwork caused the improvement. Randomization neutralizes all the variables you can't control, leaving only the variable you changed.

In machine learning and AI systems, A/B testing plays a foundational role: it is how companies validate whether a new model, feature, or design actually improves outcomes before rolling it out to everyone. Netflix runs hundreds of simultaneous experiments at any given moment, each testing something different — algorithms, artwork, recommendation rankings, UI layouts, and more.

03 — The Experiment

What the Code Does — Step by Step

01

Simulate the Users and Random Assignment

One thousand users are created, each assigned a unique ID. Each user is then randomly assigned to see either Artwork A or Artwork B — exactly as Netflix's experimentation platform assigns real members to experiment cells using a hash of their member ID. A random seed is set first to make the simulation perfectly reproducible.

02

Simulate Whether Each User Clicks

For each user, the code draws from a probability distribution: Artwork A has a true click probability of 24%, and Artwork B has a true click probability of 30%. Each user either clicks (1) or doesn't (0). In a real Netflix experiment, this is replaced by actual logged user behavior. Here, the "true" probabilities are known — the experiment's job is to recover them from noisy observed data.

03

Calculate Click-Through Rates and Visualize

The results are grouped by artwork version. For each group: total users, total clicks, and the click-through rate (clicks ÷ users) are computed. A bar chart compares the two rates visually, making the difference immediately apparent to any audience — technical or not.

04

Run a Statistical Test to Confirm the Result

A two-proportion z-test (implemented via a chi-squared test of independence) determines whether the observed difference between the two groups is statistically significant — meaning it is unlikely to be explained by random chance. A p-value below 0.05 is the standard threshold for declaring a winner.

Python 3 · Google Colab 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.patches as mpatches
from scipy import stats

# ── Experiment Configuration ───────────────────────────────────────────────
np.random.seed(42)
n_users    = 1000
click_prob = {"A": 0.24, "B": 0.30}

# ── Step 1: Assign users to artwork variants ───────────────────────────────
user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(["A", "B"], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])

# ── Step 2: Simulate clicks based on variant probability ───────────────────
clicks = np.array([
    np.random.binomial(1, click_prob[variant])
    for variant in assignments
])

# ── Step 3: Build the results DataFrame ───────────────────────────────────
df = pd.DataFrame({
    "user_id" : user_ids,
    "artwork"  : assignments,
    "clicked"  : clicks
})

# ── Step 4: Summary metrics per group ─────────────────────────────────────
summary = (
    df.groupby("artwork")["clicked"]
    .agg(
        total_users  = "count",
        total_clicks = "sum",
        ctr          = "mean"
    )
    .reset_index()
)
summary["ctr_pct"] = (summary["ctr"] * 100).round(2)

n_a      = int(summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "A", "total_users"].values[0])
n_b      = int(summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "B", "total_users"].values[0])
clicks_a = int(summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "A", "total_clicks"].values[0])
clicks_b = int(summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "B", "total_clicks"].values[0])
ctr_a    = summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "A", "ctr"].values[0]
ctr_b    = summary.loc[summary["artwork"] == "B", "ctr"].values[0]
lift     = ctr_b - ctr_a

print("=" * 48)
print("        A/B Test — Summary Results")
print("=" * 48)
print(f"\n{'Artwork':<10} {'Users':>8} {'Clicks':>8} {'CTR':>8}")
print("-" * 40)
for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    print(f"  {row['artwork']:<8} {int(row['total_users']):>8} "
          f"{int(row['total_clicks']):>8} {row['ctr_pct']:>7}%")
print(f"\n  Absolute lift (B over A): {lift*100:.2f} percentage points")
print("=" * 48)

# ── Step 5: Two-Proportion Z-Test ─────────────────────────────────────────
contingency_table = np.array([
    [clicks_a, n_a - clicks_a],
    [clicks_b, n_b - clicks_b]
])

chi2, p_value, dof, expected = stats.chi2_contingency(
    contingency_table, correction=False
)

z_score  = np.sqrt(chi2) * np.sign(ctr_b - ctr_a)
pooled_p = (clicks_a + clicks_b) / (n_a + n_b)
se       = np.sqrt(pooled_p * (1 - pooled_p) * (1/n_a + 1/n_b))
z_crit   = 1.96
ci_lower = lift - z_crit * se
ci_upper = lift + z_crit * se
ALPHA    = 0.05
significant = p_value < ALPHA

print("\n" + "=" * 48)
print("        Statistical Test Results")
print("=" * 48)
print(f"\n  Test              : Two-Proportion Z-Test")
print(f"\n  Z-Score           : {z_score:.4f}")
print(f"  P-Value           : {p_value:.4f}")
print(f"  Significance Level: {ALPHA}")
print(f"  95% CI on Lift    : [{ci_lower*100:.2f}pp, {ci_upper*100:.2f}pp]")
print(f"\n  Verdict: {'✅ SIGNIFICANT — Artwork B wins.' if significant else '❌ NOT significant.'}")
print("=" * 48)

# ── Step 6: Bar chart with significance badge ──────────────────────────────
COLORS     = {"A": "#E50914", "B": "#221F1F"}
BACKGROUND = "#F5F5F1"

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor(BACKGROUND)
ax.set_facecolor(BACKGROUND)

artworks   = summary["artwork"].tolist()
ctrs       = summary["ctr_pct"].tolist()
bar_colors = [COLORS[a] for a in artworks]

bars = ax.bar(artworks, ctrs, color=bar_colors, width=0.45,
              edgecolor="white", linewidth=1.2, zorder=3)

for bar, val in zip(bars, ctrs):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2, bar.get_height() + 0.4,
            f"{val}%", ha="center", va="bottom",
            fontsize=14, fontweight="bold", color="#221F1F")

x_mid = 0.98
ax.annotate("", xy=(x_mid, ctrs[1]), xytext=(x_mid, ctrs[0]),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="<->", color="#555555", lw=1.6))
ax.text(x_mid + 0.07, (ctrs[0] + ctrs[1]) / 2,
        f"+{lift*100:.2f}pp", va="center",
        fontsize=9, color="#555555", style="italic")

badge_text  = "p < 0.05 — Statistically Significant ✅" if significant \
              else "p ≥ 0.05 — Not Significant ❌"
badge_color = "#2E7D32" if significant else "#B71C1C"
ax.text(0.5, 0.97, badge_text, transform=ax.transAxes,
        ha="center", va="top", fontsize=9, color="white", fontweight="bold",
        bbox=dict(boxstyle="round,pad=0.4", facecolor=badge_color, alpha=0.85))

ax.text(0.5, -0.08,
        f"Z = {z_score:.3f}  |  p-value = {p_value:.4f}  |  95% CI: [{ci_lower*100:.2f}pp, {ci_upper*100:.2f}pp]",
        transform=ax.transAxes, ha="center", fontsize=8, color="#555555")

ax.yaxis.grid(True, linestyle="--", alpha=0.5, zorder=0)
ax.set_axisbelow(True)
ax.set_ylim(0, max(ctrs) * 1.35)
ax.set_xlabel("Artwork Version", fontsize=12, labelpad=8)
ax.set_ylabel("Click-Through Rate (%)", fontsize=12, labelpad=8)
ax.set_title("A/B Test: Click-Through Rate by Artwork Version",
             fontsize=14, fontweight="bold", pad=14)
for spine in ax.spines.values(): spine.set_visible(False)

legend_patches = [
    mpatches.Patch(color=COLORS["A"], label=f"Artwork A  (true p = {click_prob['A']})"),
    mpatches.Patch(color=COLORS["B"], label=f"Artwork B  (true p = {click_prob['B']})")
]
ax.legend(handles=legend_patches, fontsize=9, framealpha=0.6, loc="upper left")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("ab_test_ctr_with_stats.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()

Statistically Significant — p < 0.05

The p-value of approximately 0.025 means there is only a 2.5% chance of seeing a gap this large between the two groups if the two artworks were truly identical in performance. This is below the standard 5% threshold, so the result is statistically significant. The difference is real — Artwork B genuinely outperforms Artwork A.

The 95% confidence interval on the lift runs from roughly +0.75 to +10.65 percentage points. This means the entire plausible range of the true difference sits above zero — we can be confident B is better, even if we are less certain about exactly how much better.

05 — Business Meaning

What This Means in Plain English

The experiment showed that users shown Artwork B clicked on the title roughly 6 percentage points more often than users shown Artwork A. On a streaming platform with hundreds of millions of users, that gap is enormous. If a title gets shown to 10 million users in a day and Artwork B produces a 6-point higher click rate, that is 600,000 additional plays — per day, for a single title.

The statistical test rules out the most obvious alternative explanation: that we just got lucky and happened to see a big difference by chance. With a p-value below the standard threshold, we can conclude with confidence that the difference is driven by the artwork itself — not by randomness in who ended up in each group.

This is exactly the kind of result that drives a real business decision. The data is not saying "we think B might be better." It is saying "B is better, and here is the evidence."

06 — Recommendation

What Netflix Should Do

Business Recommendation

Ship Artwork B. Now.

The experiment produced a clear, statistically significant result. Artwork B drives a higher click-through rate, and the difference is real — not a statistical fluke. The right business decision is to make Artwork B the default for this title immediately.

But this is not the end of the story — it is the beginning of the next experiment.

Segment Before Shipping Globally

Artwork performance often varies by country, age group, or viewing history. Before a global rollout, Netflix would typically check whether the Artwork B advantage holds across all major segments — or whether it should serve different artwork to different audiences.

Watch the Full Funnel

A higher click rate is valuable only if the users who click actually watch the title. If Artwork B attracts users who click but immediately leave, the improvement is superficial. The experiment should be extended to track watch time, completion rate, and satisfaction — not just clicks.

Feed the Personalization Model

This experiment's results become training data for Netflix's machine learning models. The model learns that certain artwork features correlate with higher engagement for certain user profiles — and over time, it personalizes the thumbnail each viewer sees. Every experiment makes the model smarter.
